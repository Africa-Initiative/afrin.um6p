Why Africa Must Do More on Energy Research

March 1, 2025 Azeez Hamzat

Africa stands at a critical juncture in its development journey, where access to reliable and sustainable energy is essential for economic growth, poverty reduction, and climate resilience. Yet, the continent faces significant energy challenges that hinder progress. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), approximately 600 million people in sub-Saharan Africa lacked access to electricity in 2022, representing over 80% of the global population without electricity (IEA Africa Energy Outlook 2022). With the recent launch of the €30 million EU-AU fund to boost sustainable energy research, there’s a glimmer of hope—but Africa must do more to prioritize energy research to address its unique challenges and unlock its immense potential.

The Scale of Africa’s Energy Challenge

Africa’s energy deficit is stark. The African Development Bank (AfDB) reports that only 48% of the continent’s population had access to electricity in 2021, with rural areas particularly underserved at just 26% (AfDB Annual Report 2022). This lack of access hampers education, healthcare, and economic opportunities, perpetuating cycles of poverty. Moreover, Africa’s energy mix remains heavily reliant on fossil fuels, with coal, oil, and gas accounting for 70% of electricity generation in 2021, according to the IEA (IEA Africa Energy Outlook 2022). This reliance exacerbates environmental degradation and makes Africa vulnerable to global fuel price volatility.

Climate change further compounds the challenge. The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) notes that Africa is warming faster than the global average, with temperatures projected to rise by 3-4°C by 2100 if emissions aren’t curbed (UNEP Africa’s Adaptation Gap Report 2023). Droughts, floods, and heatwaves are already disrupting energy infrastructure, particularly hydropower, which accounts for 17% of Africa’s electricity generation (IEA). Without significant investment in energy research, Africa risks falling further behind in its pursuit of sustainable development.

The Untapped Potential of Renewable Energy

Despite these challenges, Africa has immense potential to lead in renewable energy. The continent has some of the world’s highest solar irradiation levels, with an average of 4.5 to 6.5 kWh/m²/day, making it ideal for solar power (IRENA Renewable Energy Statistics 2023). Wind potential is also significant, particularly in regions like the Horn of Africa, where wind speeds can exceed 8 m/s (Global Wind Atlas). Yet, renewable energy accounted for only 21% of Africa’s electricity generation in 2021, far below the global average of 38% (IEA). This gap highlights the need for research to develop affordable, scalable, and locally adapted renewable energy solutions.

Energy research can also address Africa’s unique challenges, such as off-grid solutions for rural areas. According to the World Bank, mini-grids and off-grid solar systems could provide electricity to 70% of unelectrified populations in sub-Saharan Africa by 2030 (World Bank Off-Grid Solar Market Trends Report 2022). However, innovation is needed to reduce costs, improve battery storage, and ensure these systems are resilient to climate impacts.

A Step Forward: The EU-AU Sustainable Energy Fund

The recent €30 million EU-AU fund to boost sustainable energy research, announced on February 27, 2025, is a welcome initiative. This fund aims to support joint research projects between European and African researchers, focusing on renewable energy technologies, energy efficiency, and solutions tailored to African contexts. It reflects a growing recognition of the need for collaborative efforts to tackle global energy challenges while addressing Africa’s specific needs. At UM6P, we are eager to contribute to such initiatives, leveraging our expertise in sustainable energy to drive impactful research.

However, while the €30 million fund is a step forward, it is insufficient given the scale of the challenge. For context, the African Development Bank estimates that Africa needs $130–$170 billion annually until 2030 to achieve universal electricity access (AfDB Annual Report 2022). Research funding must be scaled up significantly to bridge this gap and ensure that Africa can innovate its way to energy security.

Why Africa Must Lead Its Energy Research Agenda

Africa cannot rely solely on external funding to solve its energy challenges. The continent must take ownership of its energy research agenda by increasing domestic investment, fostering local innovation, and building research capacity. Currently, African countries spend an average of just 0.42% of GDP on research and development (R&D), compared to a global average of 2.2% (UNESCO Science Report 2021). This underinvestment limits the continent’s ability to develop homegrown solutions that address local needs, such as affordable solar systems for rural communities or climate-resilient energy infrastructure.

Moreover, Africa must prioritize training the next generation of energy researchers. The African Research Initiative for Scientific Excellence (ARISE) program, funded by the EU, has shown promise by providing grants of up to €500,000 to young African researchers, but only 44 fellows were selected from 1,000 applicants in its first cohort (The African Academy of Sciences). Scaling such programs is essential to build a robust research ecosystem capable of tackling Africa’s energy challenges.

A Call to Action for Africa’s Energy Future

The €30M EU-AU fund is a promising start, but Africa must do more to secure its energy future. Governments, universities, and the private sector must collaborate to increase R&D funding, support local innovation, and train energy researchers. At UM6P, we are committed to leading by example through our research in renewable energy and sustainable development, but collective action is needed to achieve transformative change. For more details on the EU-AU fund and its potential impact, read the full announcement at University World News.
